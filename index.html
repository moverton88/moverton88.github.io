Research

My PhD research focused on the origin and physiological effects of mutations in yeast. For my dissertation, I investigated the potential mutagenic effects of a CRISPR-Cas9 gene drive element when carried over evolutionary timescales. In this system, off-target cutting of the Cas9 element was detected from the genome repair processes, which generate loss of heterozygosity events. I found that current methods for LOH detection to be problematic, so I developed an improved computational pipeline to detect and analyze LOH events from short-read sequencing. I then applied this pipeline to multiple public datasets to reveal the genetic factors important for LOH rate variation in the yeast clade. Additionally, I investigated the physiological effects of mutations in yeast by reanalyzing whole-transcriptome measurements in gene deletion mutants under a simple resource partitioning model.

During my master's program, I investigated the evolutionary history and replication cycle of the extremophilic archaeal virus Sulfolobus Turreted Icosahedral Virus. From this work, I published a review on recent discoveries using this virus as a model system and its relevance to basic and medical sciences.
